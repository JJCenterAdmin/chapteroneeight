How We Will Read Together

At ChapterOneEight we do not just read the Bible — we experience it. That is why we are beginning our journey in the Gospels — the foundation of everything.

The Gospels are the first four books of the New Testament — Matthew, Mark, Luke and John. Each one was written by a different author, for a different audience, with a different perspective — but together they tell one complete story: the life, ministry, death and resurrection of Jesus Christ.

As we walk this life in faith — in hope — we have to know who we are believing in. The Gospels introduce us to Jesus — not just as a historical figure but as a living, breathing, miracle working, promise keeping Son of God. The more we know Him the more our faith — and our hope — grows. And not just grows UP — but grows DOWN.

Deep into the ground. Unshakeable. Unmovable. Impossible to uproot.

Think about the mustard seed. It is one of the smallest seeds that exists — yet it grows into one of the largest trees. But what makes it powerful is not just its height. It is the depth of its roots. Roots so deep that no storm, no wind and no force can pull it out of the ground. That is the kind of faith we are building here at ChapterOneEight.

That is why we are starting here. Not to just feel good about God — but to know Him so deeply that our faith becomes a ROOT. Something that holds us when life tries to shake us. Something that feeds us when seasons get dry. Something that cannot be moved no matter what comes.

To do this we will be using an approach called Gospel Harmony — rather than reading one Gospel at a time we will walk through all four together following the actual timeline of Jesus' life and bringing in each writer as their account aligns. Same story. Four perspectives. One complete and unshakeable picture of Jesus.

Get ready sis. Your roots are about to go DEEP.

The Gospel of Matthew

Matthew — Gift of God

Who Was Matthew?

Matthew, whose name means "Gift of God," was a Jewish tax collector also known as Levi before Jesus called him to be a disciple. As a tax collector Matthew was considered an outcast and a traitor by his fellow Jews because he collected taxes on behalf of the Roman government. Yet it was this very man — despised and rejected by his own people — that Jesus looked at and said "Follow me." Matthew left everything immediately and followed.

As a former tax collector Matthew was uniquely qualified to write an account of Jesus' life and teachings. A tax collector of that day was required to know Greek and be a literate, well organized man. Some scholars believe Matthew may have been the designated recorder among the disciples — taking careful notes of Jesus' teachings.

"Matthew nobly used his literary skill to become the first man ever to compile an account of the teaching of Jesus." — William Barclay, historian

Who Did Matthew Write For?

Matthew wrote primarily for a Jewish audience. His entire Gospel is built around proving one central truth — Jesus is the promised Messiah. He references Old Testament prophecy more than any other Gospel writer — scholars have documented over 60 direct Old Testament quotations showing how Jesus fulfilled every single one.

What Makes Matthew Unique?

  • Opens with the lineage of Jesus tracing back to Abraham — the father of the Jewish faith
  • Contains the Sermon on the Mount — the most complete collection of Jesus' teachings
  • Emphasizes Jesus as King — the fulfillment of the Jewish royal line of David
  • Ends with the Great Commission — "Go and make disciples of all nations"

Sources

  1. GotQuestions.org — Matthew in the Bible
  2. Matthew 9:9 NLT
  3. EnduringWord.com — Matthew 1 Commentary, David Guzik
  4. Catholic-Resources.org — Matthew Old Testament Quotations

The Gospel of Mark

Mark — God's Servant

Who Was Mark?

The Gospel of Mark was written by John Mark — believed by the majority of scholars to be the earliest Gospel written. Although Mark was not one of the original 12 disciples he was deeply connected to the inner circle of the early church. The early church met in his mother's home and John Mark traveled as a companion of both Paul and Barnabas. According to early church tradition, Mark wrote his Gospel using the testimony of Peter delivered in sermons — making this Gospel essentially Peter's eyewitness account of Jesus written through Mark.

Mark's journey was not without struggle. He deserted Paul and Barnabas during their first missionary journey — yet God's grace was evident in his life. Paul later commended Mark fully and by the end of Paul's life the two were fully reconciled.

Who Did Mark Write For?

In contrast to Matthew's Jewish focused approach, Mark targeted a non-Jewish audience. He wrote with a Roman reader in mind — people of action who valued power and results. This is why Mark provides explanations of Jewish customs that a Jewish reader would already know, and why his Gospel moves at a rapid, urgent pace. Mark portrays Jesus primarily as God's servant.

What Makes Mark Unique?

  • Considered by most scholars to be the earliest Gospel written
  • No birth story and no lineage — jumps straight into the ministry of Jesus
  • Most action oriented Gospel — fast paced, concise and urgent
  • Written from Peter's perspective — Peter is the first and last disciple mentioned in the text
  • Portrays Jesus as God's Servant, distinct from Matthew's emphasis on Jesus as King

Sources

  1. GotQuestions.org — Who wrote the book of Mark?
  2. Acts 13:13 NLT  ·  Philemon 1:23–24 NLT  ·  2 Timothy 4:11 NLT
  3. Mark 1:16 NLT & Mark 16:7 NLT

The Gospel of Luke

Luke — Savior of All

Who Was Luke?

The Gospel of Luke does not identify its author by name — however from the earliest days of the church, tradition has consistently attributed it to Luke, a physician and close companion of the Apostle Paul. This would make Luke the only Gentile to write any book of Scripture. Paul himself confirms Luke's role in his letters — calling him "our dear friend Luke, the doctor" and noting that "only Luke is with me."

Luke never personally met Jesus during His earthly ministry — but he conducted extensive and careful research, interviewing eyewitnesses to compile his account. He states this himself at the very opening of his Gospel: "I myself have carefully investigated everything from the beginning." He addressed his Gospel to Theophilus — described as "most excellent Theophilus" suggesting a person of honor or high rank, possibly a Roman official or wealthy benefactor.

Who Did Luke Write For?

Luke wrote with a Gentile audience in mind — emphasizing Jesus as the Savior of ALL people, not just the Jewish nation. His Gospel is unique in that it is a meticulous history — an orderly account consistent with Luke's medical mind — often giving details the other Gospels omit. Luke's history emphasizes Jesus' ministry to and compassion for Gentiles, Samaritans, women, children, tax collectors, sinners, and others regarded as outcasts in Israel.

What Makes Luke Unique?

  • Written with the precision of a physician and the heart of a historian — carefully investigated and orderly
  • Only Gentile author in the entire New Testament
  • Emphasizes Jesus' compassion for outcasts, women, the poor and the marginalized
  • Contains the most parables of any Gospel — including the Prodigal Son and Good Samaritan
  • Includes a lineage of Jesus tracing back to Adam — showing Jesus as Savior of all humanity
  • Written close enough to the events that many eyewitnesses were still alive

Sources

  1. GotQuestions.org — Summary of the Gospel of Luke
  2. Colossians 4:14 NLT  ·  2 Timothy 4:11 NLT  ·  Luke 1:3 NLT  ·  Luke 3:23–38 NLT

The Gospel of John

John — The Beloved

Who Was John?

The Gospel of John does not explicitly name its author — but at every mention of himself the author refers to himself as "the disciple whom Jesus loved." Based on the fact that he was leaning against Jesus at the Last Supper he was clearly part of Jesus' inner circle along with Peter and James. Since Peter is mentioned separately and James was martyred early in church history, that leaves John as the author — a conclusion the early church uniformly affirmed.

John was a fisherman — the son of Zebedee. He and his brother James were called by Jesus while mending their nets and left everything to follow Him. John lived the longest of all the apostles and wrote his Gospel last — after decades of deep reflection on the life of Jesus. Most scholars believe John wrote between AD 85 and 90.

Who Did John Write For?

John wrote his Gospel to persuade both believers and non-believers alike to trust in Jesus for salvation. His purpose is stated clearly: "These are written so that you may believe that Jesus is the Messiah, the Son of God, and that by believing you may have life in his name." John also wrote to address theological issues faced by a newer generation of Christians as false teachers had begun questioning core beliefs of Christianity.

What Makes John Unique?

  • No birth story, no lineage, no baptism account — begins before creation with "In the beginning was the Word"
  • Most theologically rich and reflective of all four Gospels — written after decades of contemplation
  • Contains the famous "I AM" statements of Jesus — I AM the bread of life, the light of the world, the good shepherd, the resurrection and the life, the way the truth and the life
  • Contains John 3:16 — one of the most well known verses in the entire Bible
  • Emphasizes Jesus as the Son of God — fully divine — more loudly than any other Gospel
  • Highlights 7 miraculous signs emphasizing teachings and events not in Matthew, Mark and Luke
  • Written from a place of deep personal intimacy — John was an eyewitness

Sources

  1. GotQuestions.org — Who wrote the book of John?
  2. Acts 12:2 NLT  ·  Matthew 4:21–22 NLT  ·  John 20:31 NLT  ·  John 1:1 NLT

Before You Read — A Word on Prayer

Our Bible study will be based off of the New Living Translation (NLT). Before you open your Bible I want to encourage you to always begin with prayer. Reading the Bible is not like reading any other book — it is a conversation with God. When you come to His Word with an open heart and an expectant spirit He will meet you right there on the page.

"Lord I want to learn more about You. Please lead and guide me as I read. Reveal things to me that will help me in my walk with You. Answer the questions I may have — and if not during this reading please reveal it to me soon in Your perfect timing. I trust You and I am ready to receive. Amen."
